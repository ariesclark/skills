# Writing mechanics

The sentence- and word-level moves behind [`SKILL.md`](../SKILL.md)'s bullets, plus a revision pass to catch them. Consult this when editing prose, not when drafting.

## Sentence shape

- **Nominalizations.** A verb frozen into an abstract noun hides the action: "perform an analysis of" is "analyze", "the DNA was subject to modification" is "we modified the DNA". Drill: underline every `-tion`/`-ment`/`-ance` noun and ask whether a buried verb wants out.
- **Get to the verb fast.** A long or abstract subject delays the verb and forces a re-read. Move the verb up. Before a list, give the context first ("common allergens like peanuts, shrimp, and dairy"), so length stops hurting.
- **Track the subjects.** Across a paragraph, the grammatical subjects should follow one topic. Read only the subjects; if they wander, the paragraph wanders.

## Cohesion and emphasis

- **Old before new.** Open each sentence on familiar information and end on the new part, so consecutive sentences interlock. A sentence that opens on an unfamiliar term makes the reader backtrack when the link finally arrives.
- **Stress position.** The reader emphasizes whatever lands last. Park the idea you want stressed at the end, not mid-sentence.
- **Coherence check.** A paragraph can interlock sentence-to-sentence yet still drift off topic. Read only the first and last sentence of each paragraph; if their topics don't match, it drifted.

## Passive voice, done right

Orwell says prefer active, but the passive has one real job: reordering a sentence so old information leads and new information ends. Use it to serve cohesion, not to sound objective.

- **Diagnose the noun before the voice.** When a passive sentence reads badly, the buried nominalization is usually the culprit, not the voice. "the DNA was subjected to qPCR analysis" becomes "the DNA was analyzed by qPCR": still passive, now clear.
- **Drop the actor only when it's unambiguous.** In a methods step the missing actor is often "we"; omitting it invites a misread. Cut the actor because it's obvious, never as a reflex.
- **Dangling modifiers.** An opening phrase whose implied doer isn't the clause's subject. "Using sarkosyl, the gene was shown to..." literally says the gene used sarkosyl. Name the actor: "Using sarkosyl, we showed the gene...". These come from dropping the actor, so they track over-passive prose.

## Cut and simplify

- **Ineffectual phrases, deleted whole.** "it should be noted that", "note that", "the fact that" carry no meaning.
- **Wordy to plain.** "a large number of" is "many", "due to the fact that" is "because", "prior to" is "before", "has the capacity to" is "can".
- **Inflated to simple.** "utilize" is "use", "methodology" is "method", "elucidate" is "show", "putative" is usually deletable.
- **One hedge per claim.** "may possibly suggest a putative link" stacks four; keep one.
- **No redundant modifiers.** Don't pair synonyms ("new and novel", "improved and modified") or restate the noun ("new invention", "past history").
- **Cut demeaning adverbs.** "obviously", "clearly", "undoubtedly" often paper over an argument you didn't make. Delete them, or make the argument.

## Metadiscourse

Writing about your writing ("In this section I will first... then... finally...") narrates the outline instead of delivering it. Cut the roadmap; carry the signposting in the heading and the topic sentence, which is where the reader looks.

## Dash vs. hyphen

Three glyphs, three jobs: a hyphen (`-`) joins a compound (`well-oiled`); an en-dash (`–`) marks a range (`7%–9%`, `2010–2011`); an em-dash (`—`) sets off a parenthetical. A hyphen standing in for an em-dash reads wrong. Even where an em-dash is correct, prefer a colon or a full stop: overusing it is a reliable AI tell (see [`ai-tells.md`](ai-tells.md)).

## A revision pass

Draft fast, then revise in a separate pass; editing while drafting stalls both. Revise by running mechanical checks, not a vibes re-read:

1. Underline every nominalization; free the ones hiding the real action.
2. Per sentence, name what it's about and confirm that thing is the subject.
3. Per sentence, find the link back to the previous one and check it sits at the front.
4. Per paragraph, state its point in one line; confirm the first and last sentence share that topic.
5. Read it aloud, or have text-to-speech read it; flag any sentence that loses you.
6. Hand it to a sharp reader and ask exactly where they got lost, not whether it's "good".
7. Search a kill-list ("very", "utilize", "mechanism", "framework", "it should be noted", "the fact that") and justify or cut each hit.
